Black Tuesday (1929)

Black Tuesday on October 29, 1929 is widely remembered as the climax of a violent stock-market collapse in the United States. Heavy selling pressure overwhelmed demand, and many investors who had borrowed to buy stocks faced margin calls they could not meet. Forced liquidation amplified the decline and damaged confidence across the financial system.

The crash did not single-handedly cause the Great Depression, but it contributed to a sharp tightening in financial conditions. Bank failures, weak credit transmission, and falling demand deepened the contraction over the years that followed. Policymakers and scholars later used this period to examine how market panic can spread into the real economy.

For student investors, the key lesson is that bull markets can hide fragility. Valuation discipline, diversification, and avoiding excessive leverage are foundational safeguards during euphoric periods.
