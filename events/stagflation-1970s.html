Stagflation Era (1970s)

The 1970s were marked by stagflation: persistent inflation combined with weak growth and labor-market stress. Oil shocks, policy constraints, and shifting inflation expectations made the cycle especially difficult to manage. Traditional playbooks often struggled because high inflation and low growth appeared together.

For markets, this meant volatility in stocks and bonds and a heavy focus on real returns. Investors learned that nominal gains can be misleading when inflation erodes purchasing power. Policy credibility and inflation expectations became central themes for central banks globally.

Student takeaway: track inflation-adjusted performance, understand duration risk in fixed income, and recognize how macro regimes can change portfolio behavior over long periods.
