Learning Topics

Build your investing foundation with practical vocabulary and high-impact lessons from major market events.

Vocab Vault

Definitions adapted from the Penn State Sokolov-Miller Family Financial and Life Skills Center glossary.

APR

Annual percentage rate; yearly interest charged for credit use.

Asset

Something with economic value that can produce return.

Bond

Debt instrument that pays interest and returns principal over time.

Budget

A plan for managing income, spending, and saving.

Capital Gains

Profit earned when an investment is sold for more than cost basis.

Checking Account

Bank account used for frequent deposits, withdrawals, and payments.

Compound Interest

Interest earned on both principal and previously earned interest.

Credit Score

Numeric rating of a borrower’s repayment reliability.

Diversification

Spreading investments to reduce concentration risk.

Dividend

Cash distribution from a company’s profits to shareholders.

Emergency Fund

Cash reserve set aside for unexpected expenses.

ETF

Exchange-traded fund holding a basket of securities.

FICO Score

Common credit scoring model used by lenders.

Fixed Expenses

Costs that stay about the same each month.

Inflation

Broad rise in prices that reduces purchasing power over time.

Liquidity

How quickly an asset can be turned into cash.

Mutual Fund

Pooled investment vehicle managed by professionals.

Net Worth

Total assets minus total liabilities.

Opportunity Cost

Value of the best alternative given up by a choice.

Rule of 72

Quick estimate for doubling time: 72 ÷ annual return rate.

Risk

Chance that an investment outcome differs from expectations.

Savings Account

Interest-bearing deposit account for storing cash.

Stock

Ownership share in a corporation.

Variable Expenses

Spending that can change week-to-week or month-to-month.

Historic Financial Events

Study the patterns, triggers, and lessons behind major market shocks.

Black Tuesday (1929)

What happened: A dramatic collapse in U.S. equity prices marked a pivotal moment of the Great Depression.

Why it matters: It highlights leverage risk, panic selling, and weak market safeguards.

Student takeaway: Diversify and avoid overconfidence after long bull runs.

Stagflation Era (1970s)

What happened: High inflation and weak growth challenged policy makers and investors.

Why it matters: Shows that inflation can hurt both consumers and portfolios.

Student takeaway: Learn real (inflation-adjusted) returns, not just nominal gains.

Black Monday (1987)

What happened: Major global stock indices fell sharply in a single trading session.

Why it matters: Demonstrated how liquidity stress can accelerate declines.

Student takeaway: Build rules for rebalancing and emotional discipline.

Japan’s Lost Decades (1990s–2000s)

What happened: After an asset bubble burst, Japan faced long periods of slow growth and deflation pressure.

Why it matters: Long recoveries can happen; markets do not always bounce quickly.

Student takeaway: Global diversification reduces country-specific risk.

Dot-Com Bubble (1995–2002)

What happened: Internet stocks surged on speculation, then many collapsed as valuations reset.

Why it matters: Growth stories must still connect to business fundamentals.

Student takeaway: Separate hype from sustainable revenue and cash flow.

Global Financial Crisis (2008)

What happened: Housing and credit markets broke down, causing bank failures and deep recession.

Why it matters: Leverage and interconnected financial systems amplify risk.

Student takeaway: Understand debt exposure in both households and institutions.

COVID-19 Shock (2020)

What happened: Sudden economic shutdowns caused sharp selloffs followed by fast policy-supported recovery.

Why it matters: Exogenous shocks can move markets quickly and unpredictably.

Student takeaway: Consistent investing plans can outperform panic reactions.

Inflation & Rate Cycle (2021–2023)

What happened: Inflation surged and central banks raised rates aggressively.

Why it matters: Higher rates pressure valuations, borrowing, and consumer demand.

Student takeaway: Always track macro conditions alongside company fundamentals.
